\title{Measuring the footprint of large language models in the astronomy literature}

\author[0000-0000-0000-0000]{Serat Mahmud Saad}
\affiliation{Department of Astronomy, The Ohio State University, Columbus, OH 43210, USA}

\begin{abstract}
Large language models became easy to use in late 2022, and their use in scientific
writing has grown quickly in most fields. The size of this change in astronomy has not
been reported. We measure it from the text itself. Using 200{,}547 astro-ph abstracts
posted to arXiv between 2015 and 2026, we track a set of ``marker'' words that were rare
for the whole decade before ChatGPT and then became common. The fraction of abstracts
that contain at least one such word rose from about 1.5\% before 2022 to about 5.8\% in
2025, while a matched set of neutral astronomy words stayed flat. A conservative estimate
puts a lower bound of about 4\% on the fraction of recent abstracts whose language a model
touched. We let the data pick the words and phrases without an imported list, and recover
the same style vocabulary next to the genuinely new instrument and survey names. The marker
words also appear together far more often than chance (mean pairwise lift 14, up to several
hundred for some pairs), which is what one expects if a single cause acts on the whole
abstract. Against these numbers, explicit disclosure of model use in astronomy papers stays
below 1\%, and crude checks of the LaTeX source find near-zero pasted-model text. We find no
fabricated citations in a sample of recent papers, in contrast with a rising rate in
biomedicine; the astronomy habit of building reference lists from the NASA Astrophysics Data
System appears to protect the citation record. Finally, the word signal weakens in 2026 even
as disclosure keeps rising, so word counts are a lower bound that will get harder to measure.
All data and code are public.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{Astronomy data analysis --- Scholarly communication --- Natural language processing}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The public release of ChatGPT in November 2022 made large language models easy to use, and
their use in scientific writing followed quickly. Because per-document detectors are not
reliable, recent work measures the effect at the level of a whole corpus, by tracking words
whose frequency changed abruptly after 2022. \citet{Liang2024} fit a statistical model to
arXiv, bioRxiv, and the Nature journals and report that up to about 17.5\% of computer
science abstracts by early 2024 carried model-edited text. \citet{Kobak2024} count
``excess'' vocabulary in more than 15 million PubMed abstracts and find that at least 13.5\%
of 2024 abstracts were processed by a model. \citet{Gray2025} estimate that a model was
involved in more than 10\% of all 2024 papers and describe a cat-and-mouse effect in which
words lose their signal once they become known. Physics and mathematics sit lower in these
studies, near 6--10\%.

Astronomy has been studied less. \citet{Astarita2024} count model-favored words across about
one million records in the NASA Astrophysics Data System \citep{ADS} and show a clear rise
after 2023, larger in non-refereed work; they do not convert the signal into a fraction.
\citet{GengTrotta2025} report the cat-and-mouse effect across all of arXiv. A survey of
astronomers \citep{Fouesneau2024} finds that most use these tools every week but few state so
in print. No paper has reported, for astronomy specifically, how large the footprint is, how
it compares with other fields, or how the different ways of detecting it agree.

The size of the effect matters for practical reasons. If a large share of abstracts are
edited by a model, then word-based screening of papers or proposals will flag honest authors,
and in particular non-native English speakers, at high rates. If model use is common but
rarely stated, a reader cannot tell how a given text was produced. And if models draft text
rather than only polish it, the record may drift toward a narrower and more uniform style. It
is also useful to separate three uses that are easy to run together: a model as a copy editor,
a model that drafts content, and a paper produced whole. The methods here mostly measure the
first, and a measurement of its size is a first step toward any of these questions.

We provide those numbers. We harvest every astro-ph abstract from 2015 to 2026 and measure
the marker-word signal, its rise, and its recent weakening. We convert the signal into a
lower bound on the fraction of abstracts a model touched. We let the data select the words
and two-word phrases without an imported list, and we measure how often the marker words
occur together. We then compare the word signal against three other lenses on the same
corpus: explicit disclosure in acknowledgments, pasted-model text in the LaTeX source, and
fabricated citations. We close with cuts by subfield and by author country, a measurement of
promotional versus hedging language, and the limits of the method. The point is narrow and
worth stating up front: a marker word is a sign of model-edited language, not of poor or
dishonest science.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

We queried the arXiv API for every paper with a primary or cross-listed astro-ph category,
month by month, from January 2015 through July 2026, and kept the version-1 submission date,
the primary category, and the abstract. After removing duplicate versions this gives
200{,}547 abstracts. We tokenize each abstract into lowercase alphabetic words. A word's
``document frequency'' in a year is the fraction of that year's abstracts that contain the
word at least once, which is the quantity used in earlier work.

For disclosure (Section~\ref{sec:disclosure}) and for the by-country cut
(Section~\ref{sec:geo}) we use the NASA Astrophysics Data System, whose \mw{ack} field
searches the acknowledgments section and whose \mw{full} field searches full text where
available. We count \mw{database:astronomy} records per year as the denominator.

\section{The marker-word rise}
\label{sec:rise}

We assembled a basket of marker words from earlier studies: \mw{delve}, \mw{underscore},
\mw{intricate}, \mw{pivotal}, \mw{showcasing}, \mw{meticulous}, and about thirty more. For a
control we used neutral astronomy words such as \mw{observed}, \mw{measured}, and \mw{galaxy},
which should not depend on writing tools. Figure~\ref{fig:rise} shows the two curves. The
marker basket sat near 1.5\% for the whole pre-ChatGPT decade and rose to about 5.8\% by 2025.
The control words held flat near 13\% across the same years. The rise is specific to the
model-favored vocabulary and is not a general change in how abstracts are written.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig1_markers_vs_control.png}
\caption{Fraction of astro-ph abstracts containing a marker word (red) and a matched set of
neutral control words (blue), by year. The marker basket rises after ChatGPT while the
control stays flat. Both are per-abstract fractions, so we stop at the last full year, 2025.}
\label{fig:rise}
\end{figure}

The aggregate rise hides a moving target. Figure~\ref{fig:collapse} tracks individual words
by quarter. The word \mw{delve} rose to a peak in early 2024 and then fell back toward its
starting level once it became a widely known sign of model text, while less famous words such
as \mw{underscore} and \mw{leveraging} kept rising. The total marker rate did not fall with
\mw{delve}; the load shifted to other words. Naming a marker removes it, but the underlying
habit stays, which matches the cross-field result of \citet{GengTrotta2025}.

\section{Letting the data pick the words}
\label{sec:discovery}

An imported word list invites the question of whether astronomy differs. We set the list
aside and ranked every word by how much more common it became in 2024--2026 than in
2018--2021. The top of the ranking splits cleanly into two families
(Figure~\ref{fig:discovery}). One family is the names of instruments and surveys that came
online in these years (JWST's NIRCam, MIRI, and NIRSpec; the DESI survey; XRISM), which is
what a working method should find. The other family is the same style vocabulary reported in
computer science and biomedicine, now recovered from astronomy on its own: \mw{leveraging},
\mw{pivotal}, \mw{intricate}, \mw{highlighting}. The imported list was not needed; the
astronomy text points at the same words. Table~\ref{tab:markers} lists a representative set
with their document frequency before and after ChatGPT.

\section{The marker words travel together}
\label{sec:cooccur}

If the marker words shared a common cause, they should occur together more often than chance.
For a pair of words $a$ and $b$ we measured the lift
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{lift}(a,b) = \frac{P(a \cap b)}{P(a)\,P(b)} ,
\label{eq:lift}
\end{equation}
where $P(a)$ is the fraction of 2024--2026 abstracts that contain word $a$ and $P(a \cap b)$
the fraction that contain both. A lift of one means the two words are independent. The mean
pairwise lift over the marker set is 14, and about half of all pairs exceed one
(Figure~\ref{fig:cooccur}). Some pairs are extreme: an
abstract that contains \mw{delve} is a few hundred times more likely than chance to also
contain \mw{garner} or \mw{tapestry}. Independent word drift would not produce this. A model
that edits or drafts a whole abstract, and adds several of its preferred words at once, does.
The clustering is direct support for reading the marker signal as a single effect rather than
a set of unrelated trends.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_cooccur.png}
\caption{Co-occurrence lift for pairs of marker words in 2024--2026 abstracts. A value of one
means the two words appear together as often as chance predicts. Marker words appear together
far more often than that.}
\label{fig:cooccur}
\end{figure}

\section{A lower bound on the touched fraction}
\label{sec:alpha}

To turn the signal into a fraction we count the share of abstracts that contain at least one
word from a small basket of strong, low-baseline markers, and subtract the pre-2022 rate. Let
$f_t$ be the fraction of abstracts in period $t$ that contain at least one basket word and let
$f_0$ be the same fraction in the 2018--2021 baseline. Our estimate is
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{\rm lb} = f_t - f_0 .
\label{eq:alpha}
\end{equation}
This is a lower bound for two reasons: a model-edited abstract need not keep any word from a
short list, so $f_t$ misses some model-touched abstracts; and by subtracting $f_0$ we discard
any human writer who uses a basket word for ordinary reasons. Both push the estimate down. For
2025 we find $f_t = 5.8\%$ and $f_0 = 1.5\%$, so $\alpha_{\rm lb} = 4.3\%$, and the true value
is higher.

The basket is chosen for low baseline and high specificity rather than for size; broader
baskets give larger numbers but add words with mixed meanings. As a check on the baseline, the
control words of Section~\ref{sec:rise} give $\alpha_{\rm lb}$ consistent with zero across the
whole period, and moving the baseline window by a year changes $\alpha_{\rm lb}$ by less than
half a percentage point.

Figure~\ref{fig:gap} places this number against published estimates for other fields. Astronomy
sits below computer science and biomedicine and in the same range as physics and mathematics.
Because our number is a strict lower bound while the others use fuller estimators, the honest
reading is that astronomy is in the same range as the rest of science, at a few percent to low
double digits.

fraction (about 4.3\%) next to the fraction that state model use. Right: the astronomy lower

\section{Using a model and saying so}
\label{sec:disclosure}

Authors who use a model can state so in the acknowledgments. We searched astronomy papers in
the NASA Astrophysics Data System for clear terms (ChatGPT, GPT-4, ``large language model'',
GitHub Copilot), and left out terms that are ambiguous in astronomy, such as ``Gemini'' (the
observatory) and ``Claude'' (a personal name). The count is exactly zero before 2023, which
is a good check that the search is clean, and it rises every year after
(Figure~\ref{fig:disclosure}). As a share of all astronomy papers it stays below 1\%: about
0.20\% of 2025 papers state model use in the acknowledgments and about 0.67\% mention a model
anywhere in the text. Set against the 4\% floor from the word counting, model use is common
and stated use is rare.

\begin{figure*}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{fig4_disclosure.png}
\caption{Left: number of astronomy papers per year that mention a model in full text (blue)
or in the acknowledgments (red); the count is zero before 2023. Right: the same as a share of
all astronomy papers, which stays below 1\%.}
\label{fig:disclosure}
\end{figure*}

\section{A ladder of ways to catch it}
\label{sec:ladder}

arXiv posts the LaTeX source of most papers. A model's boilerplate is sometimes pasted into
that source and left in a comment that never reaches the PDF, such as ``as an AI language
model''. We downloaded the source of 3{,}950 papers and searched for such text. The rate is
about 0.13\%, and two of the few hits are papers that study models as a research topic rather
than writing slips; the pre-2023 rate is exactly zero.

Placing the four lenses side by side (Figure~\ref{fig:ladder}) shows a range of two orders of
magnitude on the same corpus. Crude source leaks (0.13\%) and stated disclosure (0.19\%) find
the least; the word signal (4.3\%, a lower bound) finds far more. A reader who trusted only
the crude checks would conclude that astronomy has almost no model use, which is wrong. The
word counting is the only method here that sees most of the use.

\begin{figure*}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{fig9_detection_ladder.png}
\caption{Four ways of looking for model use in the same 2024--2026 astronomy papers, on a log
scale. Source leaks and stated disclosure find the least; the word signal finds most. The
biomedical estimate \citep{Kobak2024} is shown for scale.}
\label{fig:ladder}
\end{figure*}

\section{Citation integrity}
\label{sec:citations}

Models sometimes produce citations to papers that do not exist, and the rate of such
citations is rising in biomedicine, from about one paper in 2828 in 2023 to one in 458 in
2025 \citep{Topaz2026}. Astronomy has a defense: nearly every author builds reference lists
from the NASA Astrophysics Data System, so the identifiers are real by construction. We
tested this by pulling about 6{,}460 cited arXiv identifiers and 2{,}500 cited digital object
identifiers (DOIs) from the source of recent papers and checking whether each one resolves.
Every arXiv identifier resolves. Of the DOIs, the 123 that a first check flagged as missing
are all real records held in registries that the checking service does not index (arXiv's own
system, the Zenodo archive, data archives, and regional journals), not fabrications
(Figure~\ref{fig:citations}). We find no fabricated citations. The reference habit of the
field appears to protect its citation record against a problem that is growing elsewhere.

\begin{figure*}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{fig12_citation_integrity.png}
\caption{Left: the 123 DOIs a first check could not resolve are all real records in
non-Crossref registries, not fabrications. Right: the fabricated-citation rate in astronomy
(this work) against recent biomedical figures \citep{Topaz2026}.}
\label{fig:citations}
\end{figure*}

\section{Who, and where}
\label{sec:geo}

The marker rate is not uniform. By subfield (Figure~\ref{fig:subfield}), the Instrumentation
and Methods category, which is closest to software and machine learning, has the highest rate
throughout, and high-energy astrophysics the lowest. The ``computer science first'' pattern
seen across science also holds inside astronomy.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig10_subfield_diffusion.png}
\caption{Marker rate by primary astro-ph subfield. Instrumentation and Methods leads;
high-energy astrophysics trails.}
\label{fig:subfield}
\end{figure}

By author country (Figure~\ref{fig:geo}), obtained from the affiliation field, papers from
countries where English is not a first language tend toward higher marker rates, with Iran,
China, and India at the high end and Japan and Russia lower. This is consistent with a reading
of these tools as a writing aid for non-native English speakers, and it is a reason to treat a
marker word as evidence of editing rather than of anything worse. Papers from China combine a
high marker rate with one of the lowest disclosure rates, the widest gap in the sample. The
affiliation search counts every country on a paper, so large collaborations appear more than
once, and the small-sample countries are noisy; these numbers are first-order.

\section{Louder, not more careful}
\label{sec:hype}

Word counts can measure tone as well as presence. We tracked promotional words
(\mw{unprecedented}, \mw{remarkable}, \mw{striking}) and hedging words (\mw{may}, \mw{might},
\mw{suggest}) that mark caution. The promotional set rose by about 19\% over the period and
follows the marker rate closely; the hedging set did not move (Figure~\ref{fig:hype}). Abstracts
got louder without getting more careful. This is a correlation, and competition for attention
pushes promotion up on its own, but the flat caution alongside rising volume is worth recording.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering

\caption{Promotional and hedging word frequency, indexed to the 2015--2019 mean. Promotional
words rise with model use; hedging words stay flat.}

\section{The signal is weakening}
\label{sec:erosion}

The marker rate dips a little in 2026. This is not a drop in model use.
Figure~\ref{fig:erosion} places the marker rate next to the disclosure count over the same
months: the marker words soften across the board while stated model use keeps rising, already
past the full 2025 count in half a year. What weakens is the word signal, not the use. Newer
models write with fewer of the known words, and authors edit the famous ones out. Any
word-based number is therefore a lower bound, and the bound will slide down even as use grows.

\begin{figure*}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{fig8_detectability_erosion.png}
\caption{Left: the marker words weaken across the board in 2026, both the early tells that
peaked first (\mw{delve}, \mw{intricate}, \mw{pivotal}) and the later ones (\mw{underscore},
\mw{leveraging}). Right: over the same months, stated model use keeps rising. Use is not
falling; the word signal is.}
\label{fig:erosion}
\end{figure*}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The measurements above give a consistent picture. A few percent of recent astro-ph abstracts,
at least, carry model-edited language; the true fraction is higher and is in the range measured
for other physical sciences. Stated use is rare, below 1\%, so the gap between use and
disclosure is large. The marker words rise together and cluster within abstracts, which points
to a single cause rather than unrelated drift. Crude ``gotcha'' checks find almost nothing, so
the population statistics are the only reliable lens. Astronomy avoids the fabricated-citation
problem seen elsewhere, most likely because of its shared reference database.

Several limits apply. First, a marker word measures model-edited language, not model-generated
science; an abstract with \mw{delve} in it is not fraudulent. Second, for many authors, and in
particular for those writing in a second language, these tools lower a real barrier, so a
marker word is better read as a sign of editing than of misconduct. Third, the word method has
a short shelf life: as Section~\ref{sec:erosion} shows, the known words fade as they become
known, so a fixed list undercounts by more each year. Fourth, we read abstracts, not full
papers, and abstracts are the most edited part of a paper. Finally, three uses that are easy to
run together should be kept apart: a model as a copy editor, a model that drafts content, and a
paper produced whole. The word counting mostly sees the first.

These results also point to method. Word lists will keep losing power, so the more durable
signals are the ones tied to content rather than style: the co-occurrence structure of
Section~\ref{sec:cooccur}, which is harder to remove by editing single words, and integrity
checks such as the citation audit of Section~\ref{sec:citations}, which astronomy can run more
cleanly than most fields because of the NASA Astrophysics Data System.

The gap between use and disclosure has a bearing on refereeing. If a few percent of abstracts
are model-edited while under one percent say so, then a referee or an editor cannot assume
that a clean, fluent text was written without help, and a screening tool that flags marker
words will misjudge honest authors far more often than it catches misuse, because the base
rate of misuse is low and the base rate of editing is not. The same tool applied to referee
reports would face the same problem. A disclosure norm, stated once at submission and kept
simple, would remove the guesswork at low cost, and would separate the ordinary use of a model
as an editor from the uses that raise real concern.

There is also a feedback loop to watch. As a growing share of the published corpus is
model-edited, the next generation of models, and any astronomy-specific models trained on
astro-ph text, will learn from text that already carries the style. Word-frequency screening
will then measure the training data as much as the writing, and its output will drift. The
signals that do not depend on a fixed word list, such as the co-occurrence structure and the
citation checks, are the ones that will still mean what they say.

\FloatBarrier
\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

We measured the footprint of large language models in the astronomy literature from
200{,}547 astro-ph abstracts. The main results are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The fraction of abstracts with a marker word rose from about 1.5\% before 2022 to about
5.8\% in 2025, while neutral control words stayed flat.
\item A conservative lower bound puts the model-touched fraction of 2025 abstracts near 4\%;
the true value is higher and is in the range measured for physics and mathematics.
\item Without an imported list, the data recover the same style words and phrases next to the
genuinely new instrument and survey names, and the marker words occur together far more often
than chance.
\item Stated disclosure stays below 1\%, and crude source-code checks find near-zero pasted
text; the word signal sees roughly two orders of magnitude more than either.
\item We find no fabricated citations, in contrast with a rising rate in biomedicine; the
astronomy reference habit appears to protect the citation record.
\item The word signal weakens in 2026 even as disclosure rises, so word counts are a lower
bound that will get harder to measure.
\end{enumerate}
Whether astronomy should agree on a disclosure norm now, before a single case forces one, is a
question the field can still answer on its own terms.
